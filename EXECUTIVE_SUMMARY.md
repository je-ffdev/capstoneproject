Executive Summary
=================

Premature Mortality in England: Four Findings That Should Shape Policy



The Question
------------

Who dies early in England, and why? Using the Office for Health Improvement and Disparities mortality dataset, this analysis disaggregates national rates by deprivation, ethnicity and region to identify where preventable deaths are concentrated — and where targeted intervention would do the most good.


Finding 1 — Poverty kills, and it kills through the lungs.

The most deprived tenth of England dies from chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) at 4.69 times the rate of the least deprived tenth. Lung cancer follows at 3.51 times, asthma at 2.98 times. Melanoma is the only condition where the gradient reverses (0.67 times), driven by foreign holidays among the better-off. Respiratory disease is the single largest avoidable inequality in English mortality, and it is concentrated in the same places year after year.


Finding 2 — Ethnic disparities are condition-specific, not general.

Compared with the White British baseline, the Bangladeshi community shows sharply elevated mortality on a narrow set of conditions, led by diabetes. This is not a story of uniformly worse health; it is a small number of diseases doing the damage. That makes the gap actionable: targeted screening, earlier diagnosis and culturally adapted management pathways would address most of it.


Finding 3 — Deprivation does not explain the South Asian diabetes gap.

This is the most consequential result. Bangladeshi communities in the least deprived fifth of England still die from diabetes at an age-standardised rate of 379.4 per 100,000 — more than five times the equivalent rate for White British residents in the same fifth (75.5). Lifting people out of poverty would not close this gap. The drivers are structural, biological, dietary or cultural, and they require interventions that sit outside the standard "tackle deprivation" toolkit. Treating ethnicity as a proxy for poverty would leave this group behind entirely.


Finding 4 — The North–South divide is not rhetoric. It is in the data.

All-cause mortality in the North East is 2,040 per 100,000 — nearly double London's 1,058. COPD and lung cancer show the steepest regional gradients. Industrial heritage, higher smoking prevalence and concentrated deprivation in the North translate directly into shorter lives, and current regional NHS commissioning is not scaled to the size of that gap.


What It Means Together
----------------------

Inequality in English mortality is multi-dimensional. Deprivation, ethnicity and geography each carry independent signal; none is a substitute for the others. The biggest gaps cluster around preventable conditions — COPD, lung cancer, diabetes — where upstream policy has the strongest evidence base. And the gaps disappear entirely from view in national averages, which makes disaggregated reporting a precondition for honest policy.


Recommendations
---------------

1. Treat respiratory disease in the most deprived deciles as a national priority, on a par with cancer survival targets. Smoking, air quality and primary-care respiratory pathways are the levers.
2. Commission ethnicity-specific diabetes programmes that do not assume deprivation is the cause. Affluent Bangladeshi communities need them as much as deprived ones.
3. Rebase regional NHS prevention budgets against actual mortality, not population alone. The North East gap will not close on per-capita funding.
4. Make disaggregated mortality reporting standard. Headline national rates conceal four- to fivefold disparities and should never be presented alone.
